How to use this questionnaire

For the Requester / Architect

Every question must be answered before the review is submitted. "No" or "Not Applicable" requires written justification. Unanswered questions constitute a failed review, not an incomplete one.

For the Security Review Team

Use the Key Review Points as an investigation guide, not a reading list. Do not accept "Yes" without the corresponding evidence artifact. A yes without evidence is an assertion.

For the AI Governance Committee

The review output is a risk-rated deployment recommendation. Determine whether gaps are acceptable residual risks, require remediation before deployment, or are blockers preventing deployment.

Blocker — P1
Deployment cannot proceed. No exceptions without CISO and CRO sign-off. Resolution required before any production deployment. Re-review mandatory.
Required — P2
Must be remediated within 30 days of deployment. Deployment may proceed under a named risk owner with a documented remediation deadline.
Recommended — P3
Good practice gaps. Addressed in the next development cycle. Deployment proceeds. Findings logged in the AI System Register for next review.

Strategic Governance
Autonomy boundaries, risk classification, and human oversight design
BLOCKER
What is the documented boundary between autonomous AI action and human authorisation — and has it been tested at production volume, not just in a pilot?
Q 1.1
Why it matters
Without a tested autonomy boundary, the enterprise cannot demonstrate governed AI operation. A human review step that becomes a rubber stamp at production volume is not a control. Review time metrics must be evidenced, not assumed.
Key review points
The workflow document must show explicit human authorisation points. Confirm this boundary holds at production volume. Review time monitoring must exist. A document showing review steps with no evidence of review time measurement is a claim, not a control.

Architecture and Platform
Tenant isolation, complete data flow mapping, and sub-processor visibility
BLOCKER
How is tenant isolation enforced in multi-tenant platforms — independently verified by review, not just asserted by the vendor?
Q 2.1
Why it matters
Multi-tenant AI platforms create real vector bleeding risk — one tenant's query surfacing another's data. Insufficient namespace isolation exposes proprietary prompts and documents to other platform users.
Key review points
The vendor SOC 2 Type II must be read specifically for data isolation coverage — not filed. For high-risk deployments, a penetration test targeting tenant boundary isolation is required in addition to the SOC 2.

03
Data Privacy and Sovereignty
Geographic processing constraints, PII handling, and residency compliance
BLOCKER
Are all processing regions restricted to approved jurisdictions — including failover and secondary processing paths, not just the primary endpoint?
Q 3.1
Why it matters
The most common residency breach is an unexamined failover path. When the primary endpoint fails and traffic routes to a secondary region outside approved jurisdictions, the enterprise discovers this during a regulatory inquiry, not a routine review.
Key review points
Every endpoint in the failover chain must be mapped to a geographic region and confirmed against the approved jurisdiction list. Cloud tenant configuration reviewed directly — not taken on the vendor's word. No exceptions without documented legal sign-off.

Is PII sanitisation validated against actual production data types — tested for false negatives on real data, not just confirmed to work on a clean test set?

Why it matters
Masking mechanisms tested on clean synthetic data fail on real production data. Non-standard formats, multilingual content, and edge cases the NER engine was not trained on produce false negatives. A 3% false negative rate means PII passes through unmasked on 3% of every request.

Test against a representative sample of actual production data. Measure and document the false negative rate explicitly. Any false negative rate above zero requires additional controls or a Route B private pipeline architecture for that data type.

04
Vendor Risk and Compliance
Training usage, data retention, and contractual protection adequacy
BLOCKER
Does the executed MSA contain an explicit zero-training clause covering sub-processors, embeddings, and metadata — reviewed by legal, not just filed?
Q 4.1
Why it matters
A clause covering prompt content but not embeddings, metadata, or sub-processors is partial protection. Vendors frequently train on aggregated metadata even when prompt-level training is prohibited. The clause must cover every form of data the enterprise transmits.
Key review points
Do not accept a reference to the vendor's standard terms of service. The clause must exist in the executed MSA or a signed addendum. Legal must have reviewed it specifically. Watch for vague "improve our services" language — this is a training permission, not a prohibition.

05
Identity and Access Management
Credential security, RBAC implementation, and least-privilege verification
BLOCKER
Are all API keys and service credentials in an automated vault with enforced rotation — confirmed by a code repository scan showing zero hard-coded credentials?
Q 5.1
Why it matters
Hard-coded API keys are among the top sources of AI system compromise. An exposed key gives an attacker access at the enterprise's cost tier and under its contractual identity — with the ability to exfiltrate data through the model.
Key review points
A code repository scan for hard-coded credentials must be run — not assumed clean. Vault configuration must show an automated rotation schedule with rotation logs confirming execution on schedule. Any key requiring a deployment cycle to rotate is an architectural risk regardless of storage location.

Is RBAC enforced at system access, retrieval scope, and output scope levels — verified by functional testing, not just confirmed by reviewing configuration?

RBAC verified at three levels: system access, retrieval scope, and output scope. Least privilege evidenced by attempting access at a lower privilege tier and confirming restriction. Configuration review alone is not sufficient — functional testing is required.

06
Model Security and Input Controls
Prompt injection defence, system prompt protection, adversarial testing
BLOCKER
Have filtering mechanisms been tested against adversarial inputs and indirect injection through retrieved documents — not just validated on clean test cases?
Q 6.1
Why it matters
Prompt injection is the AI equivalent of SQL injection. There is no single technical fix — it requires layered controls and adversarial testing. A filter validated only on clean inputs has never been tested against the attacks it is designed to stop.
Key review points
Penetration test specifically targeting prompt injection, indirect injection through RAG retrieval, and jailbreak attempts. Must be conducted by a tester with AI-specific adversarial experience. Default content safety filter settings are not sufficient evidence of injection resistance.

BLOCKER
Is the system prompt stored in the secrets vault, injected server-side only, and tested to resist extraction through direct and indirect prompting?
Q 6.2
Why it matters
System prompts contain business logic, behavioural rules, and often backend architecture details. System prompt extraction is a documented and frequently successful attack. An extracted system prompt is a detailed map of the system's constraints and bypass opportunities.
Key review points
System prompt must never appear in front-end code, browser storage, or any client-accessible location. Code repository review confirms server-side injection only. Model must be tested to confirm it refuses to reveal its system prompt when directly asked and when manipulated through indirect prompting techniques.

Monitoring and Auditing
Log completeness, anomaly detection, forensic capability
BLOCKER
Are prompts and AI outputs logged comprehensively to a SIEM — with completeness verified by replaying a known transaction, not assumed from a dashboard screenshot?
Q 7.1
Why it matters
Without comprehensive logging, a security incident cannot be investigated, a regulatory inquiry cannot be responded to, and AI decision quality cannot be audited. This is a governance baseline for any AI system touching regulated data or making consequential decisions.
Key review points
Log completeness verified by replaying a known test transaction and confirming all required fields appear in the SIEM. PII exclusion tested — not assumed. A SIEM dashboard screenshot is not evidence of completeness. A field-by-field log schema review is.

Are anomaly detection thresholds calibrated against actual baseline usage — with a tested SOC runbook for each alert type?

08
Automated Action Limits
Hard operational thresholds, financial caps, and kill switch capability
BLOCKER
Are hard financial and operational thresholds enforced in the integration layer code — reviewed directly, not just described in documentation?
Q 8.1
Why it matters
A hallucinating AI that recommends a large transaction must be structurally incapable of executing it. The boundary must exist in the integration layer — not in model instructions. Model instructions can be overridden through prompt injection. Integration layer constraints cannot.
Key review points
Boundary logic reviewed in actual integration code — not documentation. A test run attempting to trigger an action above the threshold confirms the constraint fires. Threshold values formally approved by the business risk owner and reviewed annually — not set by the development team and left unchanged.

BLOCKER
Is the kill switch documented and tested — capable of immediate cessation without requiring a deployment cycle or an on-call developer to execute?
Q 8.2
Why it matters
When an AI system produces harmful outputs at scale, every minute of continued operation compounds the damage. A kill switch requiring a deployment, a change request, or an unavailable developer is not an emergency control. It is a process that takes time the enterprise does not have in an active incident.
Key review points
The kill switch must be tested — not just documented. Test confirms cessation within an acceptable time from trigger to cessation. Authorised activators must know who they are, how to execute, and be reachable at any hour. A kill switch in a document nobody has read is not an operational control.

09
Resilience and Availability
Failover testing, rate limit alignment, and business continuity
BLOCKER for Tier 1
Has the fallback process been tested at production volume — with the actual staff who would execute it and rate limit exhaustion as an explicit trigger condition?
Q 9.1
Why it matters
An untested fallback for a Tier 1 workload will either fail to perform adequately or have configuration gaps preventing clean activation under pressure. Rate limit exhaustion is operationally equivalent to a service outage and must be treated as a trigger condition with equal weight.
Key review points
A simulated outage test with primary model disabled confirming secondary activates, performs adequately, and the switch is logged correctly. Rate limit exhaustion tested as a separate trigger condition. Human fallback exercised with the actual staff who would execute it — not a theoretical walkthrough.

REQUIRED
Are contracted rate limits aligned with peak production load across all applications sharing the same quota — with 20% headroom above peak forecast?
Q 9.2
Why it matters
Rate limit planning based on average load is consistently the source of production AI outages. Multiple applications calling the same API simultaneously during business peak hours can exhaust limits in minutes. The enterprise discovers this on a high-volume Monday morning, not during capacity planning.
Key review points
Production volume forecasts compared against contracted quotas at peak — accounting for all applications sharing the same tier simultaneously. Queue management and retry logic reviewed for graceful degradation rather than hard failure. 20% headroom above peak forecast is the minimum baseline.
Required evidence
