Framework overview

Thirteen stages from ambition to governed enterprise scale

The framework is designed for organizations that do not yet have mature AI governance, reusable architecture patterns or a stable production operating model. Each stage has explicit deliverables and exit criteria so progress is evidence-based.

Stage 01

Establish AI Direction

Start with business strategy, not technology. Define the organizational problem, intended outcomes, sponsor, risk posture and human-accountability principles.

Core activities

Executive interviews, strategy alignment, outcome definition, stakeholder mapping, funding hypothesis and value measures.

Key decisions

Why AI is appropriate, which outcomes matter, who owns value and which uses are outside scope.

Minimum

  • Executive sponsor and business owner
  • Defined purpose and intended users
  • Human accountability statement
  • Initial success measures

Enterprise

  • AI strategy and investment roadmap
  • Portfolio prioritization criteria
  • Benefits and cost model
  • Executive governance cadence

Leading practice

  • Board-approved AI ambition
  • Enterprise value and trust scorecard
  • Scenario-based strategic planning
  • Continuous portfolio optimization
Deliverables: AI vision, sponsor mandate, strategic principles, outcome map, stakeholder register and initial roadmap.
Exit criterion: executive agreement exists on purpose, ownership, value measures and unacceptable uses.
Stage 02

Understand the Starting Environment

Assess process maturity, existing RPA and cognitive automation, data quality, integration, infrastructure, security, talent, delivery capability and adoption readiness.

Assessment domains

Leadership, data, technology, governance, skills, delivery, operations, adoption and value measurement.

Outputs

Current-state map, maturity score, dependencies, constraints and prioritized remediation.

Minimum

  • Current-state inventory
  • Critical processes and data identified
  • Known regulatory and technical constraints
  • Skills and ownership gaps

Enterprise

  • Formal maturity assessment
  • Capability heatmap
  • Architecture baseline
  • Readiness remediation plan

Leading practice

  • Automated dependency discovery
  • Quantified maturity benchmarking
  • Workforce analytics
  • Continuous readiness tracking
Deliverables: maturity assessment, capability map, dependency inventory, readiness score and remediation backlog.
Exit criterion: the organization knows what can be reused, what must be built and which gaps block safe adoption.

Stage 04

Classify Data for AI Use

Translate enterprise data classification into explicit AI treatment rules covering use, transformation, retention, residency, vendor processing, embeddings and deletion.

Minimum

  • Public, internal, confidential and restricted classes
  • Approved and blocked treatments
  • PII and sensitive-data detection
  • Named source owner

Enterprise

  • DLP and classification integration
  • Lineage, consent and purpose limitation
  • Retention and deletion rules
  • Cross-border treatment requirements

Leading practice

  • Attribute-based authorization
  • Automated masking and tokenization
  • Purpose-aware access
  • Continuous sensitive-data discovery
Deliverables: AI data treatment matrix, source register, privacy triggers, retention rules and embedding controls.
Exit criterion: every intended data class has an approved treatment and accountable owner.
Stage 05

Define Security and Infrastructure

Establish identity, environment isolation, encryption, networking, secrets, observability, resilience, incident response and sovereign deployment requirements.

Identity & Access

  • User, service and agent identities
  • RBAC/ABAC and least privilege
  • Delegated authority and step-up approval
  • Access reviews and revocation

Platform Security

  • Dev/Test/Prod separation
  • Private endpoints and approved routes
  • Encryption and key management
  • Secrets isolation and rotation

Operations & Resilience

  • Central logs and monitoring
  • HA, DR and fallback
  • Incident playbooks
  • Containment and kill switch
Deliverables: target security architecture, environment model, identity pattern, monitoring baseline and resilience requirements.
Exit criterion: security architecture owners confirm the platform can enforce the required control tier.
Stage 06

Select and Qualify AI Products

Evaluate business fit, integration, data handling, model behavior, vendor terms, subprocessors, training use, retention, cost, portability and exit.

Business & Technical Fit

  • Use-case capability
  • Integration and scalability
  • Model quality and limitations
  • Performance and cost

Risk & Contract

  • No-training clauses
  • Retention and deletion assurance
  • Residency and subprocessors
  • Audit and incident obligations

Continuity & Exit

  • Portability and fallback
  • Vendor lock-in analysis
  • Data and embedding removal
  • Replacement and transition plan
Deliverables: product scorecard, vendor risk assessment, approved-use conditions, contractual controls and exit plan.
Exit criterion: the product is approved for defined scenarios, data classes and operating conditions.
Stage 07

Establish the AI Operating Model

Define how executive, governance, delivery, assurance and operations teams make decisions and sustain the capability.

Executive Layer

  • Strategy and funding
  • Risk appetite
  • Portfolio prioritization
  • Material risk acceptance

Governance Layer

  • Use-case intake and classification
  • Policy, privacy and Responsible AI review
  • Architecture and security assurance
  • Exceptions and escalation

Delivery & Operations

  • Product ownership and implementation
  • Testing and release
  • Monitoring and support
  • Change and retirement
Deliverables: governance charter, forum mandates, decision rights, service model, support model and escalation hierarchy.
Exit criterion: every lifecycle decision has a named forum, authority, cadence and evidence expectation.
Stage 08

Define RACI, Working-Group Mandate and Stakeholder Acceptance

RACI clarifies who performs work, who owns the outcome, who must be consulted and who must be informed. The working-group mandate defines scope and authority; the escalation path defines what happens when owners disagree or controls fail.

Working-group mandate

The cross-functional working group validates requirements, resolves business/legal/technology conflicts, reviews evidence, tracks remediation and prepares recommendations for the accountable governance forum. It does not replace formal risk acceptance authority.

Escalation path

Delivery team identifies issue
→
Product and control owners attempt resolution
→
AI working group evaluates trade-off
→
Governance forum decides or escalates
→
Executive / risk authority accepts, rejects or suspends
Deliverables: lifecycle RACI, working-group charter, stakeholder acceptance record, escalation matrix and decision log.
Exit criterion: all accountable and responsible roles formally accept their obligations before implementation begins.
Stage 09

Build the AI Foundation

Implement reusable identity, data, document, prompt, model, RAG, vendor, agent, evidence and monitoring services rather than embedding controls separately into every use case.

Control Plane

  • Identity and environment resolver
  • Prompt and policy gateway
  • Scenario registry
  • Evidence and release gate

Data & Knowledge

  • Document control
  • PII masking and tokenization
  • Access-aware RAG
  • Versioned embeddings and deletion

Agents & Runtime

  • Agent and MCP registry
  • Tool authorization
  • Response validation and HITL
  • Monitoring, rollback and kill switch
Deliverables: reference architecture, reusable services, approved patterns, control mappings, test harness and operating runbooks.
Exit criterion: pilot teams can consume standardized services without bypassing required controls.
Stage 10

Implement Controlled Pilots

Select bounded, lower-risk use cases that can prove business value, adoption and control effectiveness without exposing the organization to irreversible outcomes.

Pilot Design

  • Defined users and scope
  • Approved data and environments
  • Representative scenarios and exceptions
  • Human oversight and fallback

Measurement

  • Business outcome and time saved
  • Quality and error rate
  • User trust and adoption
  • Cost and operational effort

Assurance

  • Functional and regression tests
  • Security and privacy tests
  • Adversarial and misuse tests
  • Rollback and incident simulation
Deliverables: pilot charter, test evidence, user feedback, value report, control findings and production recommendation.
Exit criterion: the pilot demonstrates measurable value and acceptable residual risk under representative conditions.

Stage 12

Monitor, Manage and Improve

Monitor not only model performance but also business value, human outcomes, access, data, vendor behavior, tool use, cost, incidents and control effectiveness.

Business & User

  • Value realization
  • Adoption and satisfaction
  • Human overrides and complaints
  • Process outcomes

AI & Data

  • Quality, hallucination and drift
  • Retrieval and citation quality
  • Data leakage and access anomalies
  • SOP and knowledge freshness

Operations & Risk

  • Availability and latency
  • Cost and capacity
  • Incidents and near misses
  • Control exceptions and recertification
Deliverables: dashboards, control metrics, incident reports, improvement backlog, recertification evidence and risk updates.
Exit criterion: the organization can detect material change, contain harm and prove ongoing control effectiveness.
Continuous knowledge change

Dynamic SOP and Operational Knowledge Governance

An SOP change can change agent behavior even when the model and application code remain unchanged. Therefore, SOP publication and agent activation must be connected but separately controlled events.

Business change identified
→
SOP approved and effective-dated
→
AI impact and affected-agent analysis
→
Re-index and re-embed
→
Regression test
→
Controlled activation
→
Monitor and retire old guidance
No confirmed and applicable SOP means no autonomous execution. The agent must stop, explain the uncertainty, preserve evidence and route the case to a qualified person.
Required controls: named SOP owner, approved source repository, version and effective dates, dependency register, semantic comparison, re-embedding pipeline, regression suite, controlled activation, rollback and evidence of the exact SOP used.
Evidence and assurance

Reconstruct every material AI decision or action

For regulated or high-impact use cases, the organization must be able to explain what happened, why it happened, which controls were applied and who approved the outcome.

Identity & Context

User or event, role, environment, scenario, purpose and authorization.

AI & Knowledge

Model, prompt, policy, SOP version, sources, retrieval, embeddings and tool calls.

Decision & Outcome

Control decisions, validations, human approvals, final action, monitoring result and incident linkage.

Deliverables: evidence catalogue, retention schedule, immutable audit design, decision reconstruction procedure and regulatory response process.
Stage 13

Scale, Industrialize and Retire

Scale through reusable patterns, shared platforms, federated governance and continuous assurance. Retire capabilities completely—not only the user interface.

Scale

  • Reusable reference patterns
  • Shared control-plane services
  • Federated delivery standards
  • Portfolio and value management

Industrialize

  • Automated testing and evidence
  • Policy-as-code
  • Continuous assurance
  • Capability and workforce development

Retire

  • Disable execution and triggers
  • Revoke identities, MCP and tools
  • Remove embeddings and vendor data
  • Retain required evidence and verify closure
Deliverables: scale roadmap, reusable service catalogue, federated governance model, recertification process and retirement checklist.
Exit criterion: growth does not weaken accountability, control consistency or auditability.
