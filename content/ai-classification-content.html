AI Classification, Change & Assurance

A practical model for deciding what kind of AI is being adopted, how difficult it is to introduce safely, which risks apply, what technology and controls are required, how changing SOPs affect agents, and how evidence and retirement are managed.

2 · Combined risk decision

Risk is determined by the complete operating context

AI type × data sensitivity × business impact × autonomy × external exposure × reversibility

Low

Internal assistance, approved information, no material decision and human validation.

High

Confidential data, customer-visible content, regulated advice, integration or difficult-to-reverse outcomes.

Critical

Autonomous action, financial or legal impact, highly restricted data, MCP execution, multi-agent delegation or limited reversibility.

3 · Dynamic SOP governance

Agents must remain reliable while instructions change

An SOP change can change agent behaviour even when the model and application code do not change.

Business change
→
Approved and effective-dated SOP
→
AI impact and affected-agent analysis
→
Re-index / re-embed
→
Regression test
→
Controlled activation
→
Monitor and retire old guidance
No confirmed and applicable SOP means no autonomous execution. The agent must stop, explain the uncertainty and route the case to a qualified person.
4 · Change taxonomy and ownership

Every behaviour-changing dependency is governed

Changes in scope

  • Model, prompt and safety policy
  • SOP, knowledge source and business rule
  • Embedding model, chunking and index
  • MCP server, tool description, permission and API schema
  • Data source, threshold, orchestration and approval route

Accountable roles

  • Business process and SOP owner
  • AI product or agent owner
  • Knowledge and index owner
  • Technology and security owner
  • Risk, legal, privacy and compliance approver
  • Operations and monitoring owner

Emergency changes require a named authority, reason, affected scope, effective and expiry times, temporary controls, heightened monitoring, rollback and post-event review.

5 · Evidence, rights and supply chain

Every material decision must be reconstructable

Decision evidence

User or event, identity, scenario, model, prompt, SOP, sources, retrieval, tool calls, control decisions, approvals, final action and monitoring result.

Affected-person rights

Disclosure, understandable explanation, challenge, human reconsideration, correction, complaint path and response timeline.

AI supply chain

Model and dataset provenance, licences, AI bill of materials, open-source integrity, MCP/tool registry, subprocessors and IP controls.

6 · Retirement and exit

Decommission the capability, not only the interface

Disable

Stop execution and revoke users, service identities, agents, MCP servers, tools, APIs and triggers.

Remove and retain

Delete or archive prompts, configurations, embeddings and vendor-held data while retaining required evidence.

Validate

Confirm no residual action path remains, notify users and downstream systems, and activate replacement or fallback processes.

Canadian regulatory clarification

Use current law and sector-specific obligations

The former federal Bill C-27/AIDA proposal did not become law. Canadian AI governance should be mapped to applicable privacy, human-rights, consumer-protection, sectoral, administrative-law and public-sector requirements. Cross-border processing is not universally prohibited by PIPEDA; organizations remain accountable for comparable protection and must comply with applicable contractual, sectoral and organizational residency rules.
